Our Top 5

Here at Visit GA, our goal is to help you and your family plan your getaway in the gem of a state Georgia is. Here is our list of the top five must see state parks throughout the Peach State!




1 / 5

#5: Indian Springs State Park, Flovilla, GA

Take a journey through time to explore one of the oldest state parks in the whole nation. Indian Springs is over 500 acres with a 105 acre lake and plenty of biking trails, fishing spots, and more. Here you can find over 60 different campsites as well as group and picnic shelters available for reservation. There is also a museum here that can show you the historical past of the area such as how, centuries ago, water from the springs was used by the Creek Indians medicinally which started the deep-rooted belief of its healing powers. Today, you can still find people drinking the mineral-rich water Indian Springs has to offer.

2 / 5

#4: Stephen C Foster State Park, Fargo, GA

Our number four spot goes to Stephen C Foster State Park and its location in the infamous Okefenokee Swamp. Stephen C Foster State Park has a lot to see with it being in the largest wetland in the south. It is located within a National Wildlife Refuge that showcases a diverse ecosystem with Spanish moss coating the trees and an abundant wildlife roaming around. Here travelers and photographers can see the breath-taking scenery the Okefenokee Swamp has to offer but don't be alarmed if you see an American Alligator swimming around, as it is one of the most famous inhabitants of the Okefenokee Swamp. You can also find a variety of different birds like herons, wood storks, red-cockaded woodpeckers, and more. Even black bears might make an appearance on your visit so be sure to admire these animals from a distance and practice safe actions while you boat around. There are nine cottages and over 60 campsites the park has to offer as well as three picnic shelters making it a great location for adventurers and families to explore one of Georgia's natural seven wonders.

Explore Stephen C Foster State Park more here!

3 / 5

#3: Providence Canyon State Park, Lumpkin, GA

Providence Canyon State Park earns the third spot on our list. Providence Canyon is known as Georgia's "Little Grand Canyon" but is by no means little. Providence Canyon State Park has an elevation of over 500 feet and miles of trails that will not fail to amaze you! Hikers and park goers will have the chance to view the canyon from above and below. The canyon's soil dons orange, red, pink, and purple hues that make a beautiful, natural canvas to explore. Beyond this, there are two picnic shelters available for reservation as well as nine different campsites to choose from. There's also a museum with many exhibits to see as well as a visitor center to welcome you to the park. If you plan on exploring Georgia's "Little Grand Canyon", it's best to bring plenty of water as well as a change of clothes if you plan to explore the park to it's fullest.

Explore Providence Canyon State Park more here!

4 / 5

#2: Cloudland Canyon State Park, Rising Fawn, GA

In our number two spot is Cloudland Canyon State Park, located in northwest Georgia on the western edge of Lookout Mountain. With an elevation 1,980 feet, Cloudland Canyon offers a beautiful view for anyone willing to make the trek to the top. The stunning waterfalls, thousand-foot canyons, creeks, cliffs, and wildlife make Cloudland Canyon one of the most schenic places in the state. There's 30 miles of trails for hikers and bikers as well as caves for park goers to explore. Here at Cloudland Canyon, families can stay in one of the 90 different campsites and cottages the park has to offer and enjoy playing disc golf, fishing, picnicking, or going horseback riding on the miles of trails that are there to explore. We could go on and on about the beauty of Cloudland Canyon and all it has to offer but we'll leave it for you to see yourself!

Explore Cloudland Canyon State Park more here!

5 / 5

#1: Tallulah Gorge State Park, Tallulah Falls, GA

Stealing our number one spot is one of the most canyons in the eastern U.S., Tallulah Gorge State Park. Two miles long and 1,000 feet deep, Tallulah Gorge offers miles of strenuous trails, rock climbing, and kayaking to its many visitors. Hikers can also go over a suspension bridge that sways 80 feet above the jagged terrain below. Those that go over the bridge will have an incredible view of the waterfalls and river below. In total, there are five stunning waterfalls for hikers to explore at Tallulah Gorge. Tallulah Gorge also offers over 50 campsites and many spots for picnicking aswell as places to swim or go whitewater rafting. There is also a sand beach, gift shop, and tennis courts for visitors to do and see. Those that plan on exploring Tallulah Gorge should dress and prepare appropriately with the right shoes and bringing plenty of water. Hikers should also beware of the slippery rocks and slopes and remain careful when trekking through Tallulah Gorge State Park.
